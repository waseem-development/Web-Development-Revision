**1) What is AAOIFI?**

AAOIFI stands for Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions. Think of it like an international committee of Islamic finance scholars and experts based in Bahrain. Their job is to set the official rules for what makes something halal in finance. When we say our stock screener uses "AAOIFI standards" — it means we are using the same rules that Islamic banks worldwide use. It gives our screener credibility because we are not making up our own rules — we are following the globally accepted Islamic finance rulebook.

**2) The three transparency methods in simple words**

Imagine you give ₨10,000 to a charity through our app. How do you know it actually arrived? Here are the three ways we can prove it:

Method A is like an ATM receipt. When you withdraw money from an ATM, the machine prints a slip saying "transaction successful." Raast does the same thing digitally. When the money leaves your account and arrives at the NGO's account, the bank system sends our app a confirmation message with a reference number. We show that reference number on your receipt. You can even call your bank and they will confirm that transaction reference exists. Simple, free, and works immediately.

Method B is like a school teacher asking students to show their homework. After the NGO receives the money, they have 7 days to log into our admin panel and upload photos, receipts, and a short update showing how the money was used. If a flood relief NGO received ₨500,000 they must upload photos of the food packages distributed, the receipt from the supplier, and a short description. If they do not upload within 7 days, the app automatically pauses their campaign so no new donations can come in. This forces accountability.

Method C is like writing something in stone. Blockchain is a technology where every transaction is recorded permanently on thousands of computers around the world simultaneously. Nobody can change or delete it. Think of it like a public noticeboard that nobody owns and nobody can edit. The problem is it is very expensive, very complex to build, and completely unnecessary when Method A and B already prove the money arrived and was used correctly. We skip this for now.

**3) The stock screener explained like you are explaining to a friend**

Imagine you have ₨50,000 and you want to buy shares in a Pakistani company. But you are Muslim and you want to make sure the investment is halal. You open our app, search for "PTCL" and here is what happens behind the scenes in about two seconds.

First check — what does this company actually do? PTCL provides telephone and internet services. That is completely fine Islamically — no alcohol, no gambling, no weapons. It passes the first check automatically.

Second check — how is the company's money structured? This is where it gets interesting. Even a halal business can become problematic if it has borrowed too much money from interest-based banks. Imagine a halal restaurant that borrowed so much from a bank that most of its value is actually bank debt. That debt is creating interest somewhere in the system. So we check four numbers from PTCL's financial report that they publish every three months:

How much debt does PTCL have compared to its total market value? If it is more than 33% of the company's value, there is too much interest-based debt in the picture.

How much of PTCL's income comes from interest? For example if PTCL keeps ₨10 billion in a conventional bank and earns interest on it — that interest is haram. If this interest income is more than 5% of their total revenue, it fails.

How much money do people owe PTCL? If most of their assets are actually just unpaid bills from customers, the business is too dependent on credit, which can involve interest elements.

After both checks, the app gives one of three answers. Halal means the company passed everything — you can invest with peace of mind. Doubtful means it passed the business check but is very close to the limit on one of the financial numbers — a scholar might say yes, another might say no, so we warn you and suggest consulting your imam. Haram means it failed — either the business itself is forbidden or the financial structure has too much interest involvement.

The user then adds PTCL to their watchlist. Three months later when PTCL publishes new financial results, our app automatically re-runs all the checks. If anything changes, the user gets a notification on their phone.

**4) Corporate CSR explained simply**

CSR stands for Corporate Social Responsibility. In Pakistan, companies — especially listed companies and banks — are legally and socially expected to spend money on public welfare. Think of it like a company's obligation to give back to society.

Here is a real world example. Imagine a medium-sized Pakistani textile company called ABC Fabrics. Their board of directors says "we need to spend ₨600,000 this year on CSR — something that looks good, is Islamic, and we can show our shareholders." They go to a consultant who charges them huge fees to manage a random charity event. Nobody really knows where the money went.

Now with our platform, ABC Fabrics pays us ₨50,000 per month. We create a branded page for them — "ABC Fabrics Flood Relief Campaign." Their employees can donate and the company matches every employee donation. Every rupee is tracked transparently. The NGO posts photos. ABC Fabrics gets a monthly PDF report showing exactly how much was donated, to which cause, with photos of impact. They show this to their shareholders and it looks genuinely credible because scholars certified the NGOs.

For us it is ₨50,000 per month per company. For them it is a complete managed CSR solution that is Shariah-certified and fully transparent. Everyone wins.

**5) What are CSR, EMI, SECP and SBP — all explained simply**

CSR — Corporate Social Responsibility. Companies spending money on society. Explained above.

EMI — Electronic Money Institution. This is a special license from SBP that allows a company to create and manage digital wallets where users store actual money. JazzCash has this license. SadaPay has this license. Without it you cannot legally hold customer money in a digital account. It requires ₨200 million minimum capital. We do not need this for Phase 1 because we never hold money — donations go directly from donor to NGO.

SBP — State Bank of Pakistan. The central bank. Think of it like the government department that controls all banking and payment rules in Pakistan. Every payment app, every bank, every wallet needs their approval.

SECP — Securities and Exchange Commission of Pakistan. The government body that regulates investing, stock markets, and companies. When you register a private limited company, that is with SECP. When you want to legally offer investment products, you need SECP approval.

NBFC — Non-Banking Finance Company. A company that does financial services like loans and investments but is not a bank. To offer microloans or crowdfunding, we eventually need SECP to register us as an NBFC.

Raast — Not a company or a license. It is SBP's free instant payment system — like Pakistan's version of India's UPI. It lets money move between any two bank accounts instantly for free. We use this to transfer donations directly from donors to NGO bank accounts.
